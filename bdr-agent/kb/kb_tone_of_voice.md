# Tone of Voice & Style

## Style
- Business casual
- Professional but natural
- Concise and direct
- Not overly polished or corporate
- No hype, fluff, or salesy language
- No long intros or filler phrases
- Short, punchy sentences — one idea per sentence
- Clear spacing between paragraphs — each paragraph is one thought
- Easily scannable — a rep should be able to read it in 10 seconds
- Avoid verbose or run-on sentences; break them up instead

## Writing rules
- Always keep pronouns — never drop "I", "we", "you"
  - Bad: "Put together this PDF for you"
  - Good: "I put together this PDF for you"
- End with exactly one short CTA question (maximum 2 questions total in the message) — see CTA rules below. Skip the question entirely if the prospect has already booked a meeting; in that case a simple confirmation like "Looking forward to chatting" is enough.
- Focus on the lead's goals, priorities, or pain first — then on Albato's features
- Avoid pushy or pitchy language
- If the lead's writing style was detected, lightly adapt cadence and energy to match — do not copy their phrasing verbatim
- **Never use em dashes (—)**; use a comma, period, or rewrite the sentence instead

## CTA rules
- One short closing question maximum
- Ask about their situation, priorities, or interest — not "do you have 15 minutes?"
- Examples of good CTAs:
  - "Is this something you're actively looking at?"
  - "Would it make sense to dig into this together?"
  - "What does your current integration setup look like?"

## Banned phrases
Never use any of the following — in openings, follow-ups, or anywhere in the message:

### Hollow openers / well-wishes
- "I hope this email finds you well."
- "Hope you're doing well."
- "Hope all is well."
- "Hope you're having a great week."
- "Hope you had a great weekend."
- "Hope your week is off to a great start."
- "Hope things are going well."
- "Hope everything is going smoothly."
- "Hope you've been well."
- "Hope you're keeping well."

### Generic follow-up clichés
- "Just checking in."
- "Just following up."
- "Following up on my previous email."
- "Wanted to follow up."
- "I wanted to circle back."
- "Circling back on this."
- "Touching base."
- "Checking back in."
- "Reaching out again."
- "Quick ping."
- "Friendly reminder."
- "Gentle reminder."
- "Nudging this thread."
- "Bumping this to the top of your inbox."
- "Bringing this back to the top of your inbox."

### Inbox-related clichés
- "Wanted to make sure this didn't slip through the cracks."
- "Hope this didn't get buried."
- "Hope this didn't get lost in your inbox."
- "In case you missed my previous email."
- "Not sure if you saw my last note."
- "Resurfacing this."
- "Floating this back up."
- "Moving this to the top of your inbox."

### Permission-seeking fluff
- "I know you're busy, but..."
- "I know things can get hectic."
- "I know you have a lot on your plate."
- "I realize you're probably swamped."
- "When you have a moment..."
- "If you get a chance..."
- "Whenever you have a minute..."

## Training additions
- Always address the prospect by first name only, never their full name (e.g. "Hi Eden" not "Hi Eden Halperin").
